## Page 1 — Main sheet

### Header

- **Type**: IDEATION SHEET — EXPLORATION ACTIVITY
- **Title**: WikiYouthBot: Discovering the Personalization of an AI
- **AI exploration phase**: P3 — Validation and Experimentation on AI
- **Duration**: 5-day intensive session (modular: each step can also be run standalone)

### AI and Exploration Topics

- **Custom AI projects**: how a generative AI can be specialized on a local topic through system prompt and knowledge base.
- **Datasets**: how a structured knowledge base shapes the answers of an AI.
- **Source verification and media literacy**: how to evaluate facts versus AI hallucinations, by cross-checking with trusted institutional sources.
- **Personas and tone**: how an AI adapts to different users through explicit user profiles.
- **Bias and limits of AI**: how to make the chatbot say "I don't know" instead of inventing.

### Materials Needed and to Create

**Shared:**
- 30-card SDG bingo wall (situations linked to SDGs)
- Drawing bag with 30 numbered tokens
- 3 pre-configured AI projects (Claude Project, Gemini Gem or GPT custom)
- Central voice AI device for the press conference
- Sources dictionary (UN, WHO, IPCC, FAO, ILO, UNESCO, UNICEF, Eurostat, etc.)
- Kanban progression board on the wall

**Per group (3 groups recommended):**
- 1 laptop with access to its own AI-project
- 3 printed bingo grids + erasable markers
- Persona card templates (printable, A5)
- A3 paper or foam board for the visual campaign
- 1 archive box (cardboard, crate or container) for the dataset
- Cards in colored Bristol (blue, green, yellow, orange, red frame)
- Smartphones or simple sensors for field measurements
- Camera, notebooks, pens for the field investigation

### Exploration Steps and Description

This intensive session lets youngsters build their own specialized chatbot, anchored in a local SDG cause. They face a generic AI at the start and turn it into "theirs" by adding bricks step by step: cause, verification rules, quality requirements, dataset, personality, personas, mascot.

**Intro — SDG Bingo.** Players discover the 17 SDGs through a 30-card bingo. Each card describes a real-world situation linked to one or several SDGs. After the game, each participant places 5 stickers on the photos that struck them the most. The resulting heatmap is used to form groups by topic.

**Step 1 — I Heard a Rumor.** Each group picks a SDG cause, starts a sensitization campaign on paper, and verifies 3 rumors launched by the facilitators. Each rumor is cross-checked by one pair using their (still empty) chatbot, and another pair using official sources. *Brick added: thematic specialization, first verification rules.*

**Step 2 — The Press Conference.** Groups prepare 10 questions (5 general, 5 local) on their cause. They ask them in parallel to a central voice AI and their individual written chatbots. Inconsistencies are caught and challenged in journalistic style. *Brick added: quality requirements (sources, dates, named actors).*

**Step 3 — The Data Box.** Groups go out for a field investigation in their neighborhood, then complete with documentary research. They produce a physical archive box, organized in colored categories (actors, figures, citations, photos, Q&A). The box is then uploaded to their AI-project. *Brick added: full dataset anchored in the local territory.*

**Step 4 — The Chatbot Studio.** In parallel sub-workshops, groups give their chatbot a written personality, define 2 to 3 personas, and design a mascot. They run cycles of tests and adjustments to stabilize the chatbot's tone and limits. *Brick added: personality, personas, mascot, tested behavior.*

**Conclusion — Neighborhood Field Test.** Groups present their visual campaign and their chatbot to a real audience (visitors, partners, micro-trottoir, or peer test). Feedback is collected and one last adjustment closes the session. *Brick added: final stabilized version, ready to be shared.*

### Expected Output

By the end of the session, each group has produced:
- A defined SDG-anchored problematic with a local angle.
- A complete dataset of their cause (proofs, actors, figures, citations, Q&A cards).
- A working text-only chatbot, able to carry a sensitization message with explicit limits.
- A physical sensitization campaign poster, including the chatbot's mascot.

### The Hook and the Playfulness

Youngsters don't theorize about AI. They build it. They face a generic chatbot at the start and watch it become theirs, brick by brick. Each step is a stage where the chatbot gains a new ability, like leveling up a video game character. They confront AI hallucinations in a journalistic press conference, verify rumors like detectives, and explore their neighborhood to collect real evidence. The week ends with a public confrontation of their creation.

### Success Indicators

- Each group can explain in their own words how an AI gets specialized (system prompt, dataset, personas).
- Each chatbot answers consistently on its cause and says "I don't know" when needed, rather than inventing.
- Each group can name the local actors, figures, and sources that fed their dataset.
- The chatbot adapts its tone and length to different personas during testing.
- Final visitors describe the chatbot as useful, local and trustworthy.

## Page 2 — Modular standalone mode

The session is not tied to a fixed duration. Each step can be deployed standalone in shorter contexts (single classroom slot, partner intervention, school visit). The progression by collective milestones remains the readable backbone.

### Step-by-step standalone objectives

| Step | Standalone objective | Outline | Preparation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SDG Bingo** | Discover the 17 SDGs in a playful way. | Play a classic bingo round, then conclude with an open discussion on the situations that struck the group most. | SDG bingo wall, grids, tokens, stickers. |
| **I Heard a Rumor** | Discover a societal issue critically and practice source verification against AI. | Pick a cause, explore hypotheses on a poster, verify 3 launched rumors by cross-checking AI answers with official sources. | Propose one or two causes, prepare the 3 rumors, check AI and source access. |
| **The Press Conference** | Train question quality and answer comparison across several AI models. | Prepare questions on a chosen topic, ask them in parallel (voice + text), observe divergence, approximation and contradictions. | Provide a topic, prepare a note-taking template, check voice device and access to written chatbots. |
| **The Data Box** | Document a local issue through field investigation and confront the dataset to an AI. | Field walk in the neighborhood, complementary desk research, structured archive box, confrontation with an AI to see what it knows, invents and misses. | Choose a topic and a perimeter, prepare 3 to 4 starter questions, gather box material, AI access at end of session. |
| **The Chatbot Studio** | Embody a chatbot and stabilize its behavior against varied questions. | Give a personality, personas and a mascot to a generative AI in project mode, then test and adjust in short cycles. | Pre-configure an AI project with a topic and starter dataset, accessible at session start. |

### Notes for facilitators

- The collective bingo step is not tracked on the kanban; the kanban starts at step 2 when groups are constituted.
- The chatbot of each group is hosted in the structure's account, not in a facilitator's personal account, to ensure access continuity between sessions.
- Final chatbots are accessible via a public link, posted as a QR code on the visual campaign.
- All productions (chatbot, dataset, persona cards, campaign) are kept identifiable across sessions and feed the next stages of the YouthAiLab year-long thread.
